The Out of Office Application

Introduction

At ICAEW managers and employees were wasting time in their day trying to find people who may not even be working that day. There was no clear uniform way to quickly track if an employee was absent, on holiday, training or working from home. This created confusion and wasted precious time that could have been better spent on projects that were of a high priority. Myself and another apprentice were tasked with completing an Out of Office application to solve this problem. It was our first company project and we would have to plan, design, develop and maintain the application ourselves.

The Project team consists of:

  • 1 Product Owner
    • Has a vision of what he or she wishes to build, and conveys that vision to the scrum team.
  • 1 Project Manager
    • The project management role is one that is designed to help ensure that the software development process works as it is intended.
  • 1 Scrum Master
    • Manages how information is exchanged, runs the team and helps clear any blocked tasks.
  • 2 Developers - My Role
    • Codes the application to the specifications of the designs and clients requirements. Upon completion of work, maintain the site and fix bugs for the until the app is discontinued.
  • 2 Testers
    • Testing the application once it has been completed to find bugs and flag them up to the developers.

Planning and Requirement Analysis

I found this phase very interesting as it allowed me to gain insight into the inner workings of the company and exposed me to the software development lifecycle. Myself and another apprentice began by meeting with the product owner and informed about our developer roles on the project. The high concept problems were presented to us and together we asked questions to better understand what the client wanted. I began with enquiring about the app functionality and what data we would need to collect from users. The client wanted users to be able to book holiday and anything relating to being out of the office through the app, as well as letting other users see which of their co-workers was absent. After our initial enquiries we agreed that we would need to have a login system, employee tracking, users grouped by their current work teams and approvers to validate any time off requested.

Defining Requirements

After all the meetings and conferring with the scrum master, we constructed a list of requirements that would serve as the scaffolding for our overall project. This included things such as “Access and Display” that focussed on the front end and initial login. The functionality of “Making Requests”, specifying how users can interact with the site through the UI, the varying amount of time they can book off and receiving automated emails. I also noted information that we collected for approvers, and what their abilities would be such as who can be an approver and selecting the different request types they can approve. We covered as much as possible with our documentation, to ensure that we produced the best possible product in an effective and inclusive way for everyone involved. Using the requirements, we created small easy to digest tasks that clearly reflected the work and the time required to complete them.

Together with our product owner we estimated that the work should be completed in four months. The third quarter of 2018 would be our deadline to create a fully functioning and tested application. These were my tasks for creating a meaningful dashboard. Most of these were primarily to do with HTML, Jquery and Fetching data.

Throughout the project we used burndown graphs such as this one, to convey how much time had been lost due to uncompleted tasks. This allowed us to have a clear visual representation of how well we were progressing, whilst creating an underline sense of urgency to finish work in a timely manner. As you can see in this graph, we were on target but in the last few days of that sprint some tasks were added. These tasks were tester related and were created to resolve raised bugs that I will reference later on.

Designing the Product Architecture

The next step was the Design phase where myself and the other apprentice worked on the basic flow and structure of the application. This allowed the team to collectively see how the app would work and what the happy uninterrupted paths were. The diagram below highlights all the key directions the user could potentially take when requesting time out of the office. It helped to show what parts of the application that are crucial for a user to progress with their request. Examples of this are the login section to know which user is currently applying for requests. The manager that is currently working to function as the approver for that team. If the users wanted to cancel or edit a request, creating a new request and whether it even requires approval.

This highlighted what information we would need to collect from the user, helped us to create the tables and stored procedures in our database. While providing the team with a clear visual representation of how the application will function at a basic level.

I took on the tasks for the front end of the application, which was coded using HTML and Jquery. I began by designing the layout of the landing page that users are initially directed to on paper. This included the style, colour coding, separation of events and information to be included within those events. I highlighted how different requests will have statuses that indicate if they are approved or waiting to be approved. I included clear indications If the event is a full day out of the office, AM or PM absence. Even at this stage we were planning click functionality to allow users to right click on events and have a context menu for quick tasks. This included going to the pending request page to see which events were yet to be approved and making a request directly on the calendar, instead of going to fill out a request form on a different page.

I presented this design to the product owner and the other developer and took on board the comments that they provided. I added the standard company header and logo, then the contact page. Next the company CSS (Cascading Style Sheet) was added; It is used on all the applications to create a uniform brand. When designing the app, we decided to split the users into their ICAEW teams because it would make the calendar less cluttered and easier for their team leader to manage. Some employees within the company, have tasks that heavily rely on other workers before they can be completed. These uncompleted tasks can potentially cause bottlenecks and stall progress which costs us time and money. We recognised this and included the ability for a few select users to filter other teams’ calendars, as well as their own. After checking the calendar, users can see if any key employees are not available that sprint, and then decide which current tasks would take priority.

Building or Developing the Product

Database

Myself and the other developer worked together to create the database for the application. We each chose the sections we would create for our separate tasks; we regularly checked in with one another to ensure that our work was uniform and up to ICAEW standards. With the information and notes I previously gathered from the happy path diagram, I created the table for the user’s absence requests. This included selecting which fields could potentially be null (have no input data from the user) and which fields must include input data.

Once we had our tables, we created the CRUD stored procedures that would function as our way to populate the database with all the required information from users.

  • Request Type Id
    • Represents different types of leave (Holiday, Working from Home, Training and Time Off in Lieu)
  • Status
    • If the request has been Approved, Pending or Declined.
  • User Comment
    • Any added information from the user.
  • Availability Status
    • Is the user available to work? Holiday would be no. Training no and Working from home is yes.
  • Availability Comment
    • Any added information about availability.
  • Reviewer User Id
    • Who approved or declined the request.
  • Review Date
    • The date it was approved or declined.
  • Reviewer Comment
    • Any additional information from the approver. Potentially a reviewer could specify why they declined a request e.g. “That date is unsuitable for you to be out of the office as this is an important release date and we require you to oversee the work”.

Including the “Last Modified By / Last Modified Date” fields allowed greater traceability for our requests as we could determine how recent the request was made, when the last edit was and who approved it. The next step was designing the other tables that would allow us to separate users into clear, easy to define groups and teams. The first table was simply called “User” that contained information about that employee, such as their name, email and job position. In the table was also their Id which was a primary key and unique to every user. The next table was “Team” and I linked the job position from the first table as a foreign key in this one, to assign employees to their respective teams. Individual teams also had an Id and was done to allow me to easily filter through them on the calendar.

Entities and Data Access

The other developer setup the base data access layers and the database connection. I created the access layers specific to the work that I was doing with the requests. I utilised the connection and linked the stored procedures that I had previously included in the database to this absence request command repository.

Each repository in the data access layer will have a corresponding interface which will then act as a contract for the repository. Each of the repositories will also inherit from the BaseRepository which will contain the basic methods needed to complete the CRUD functions. By including the interfaces within the projects, it then means that the code does not need to be duplicated for each one meaning that development time can be saved and fewer mistakes are likely to happen. This is the beneficial to the business because it means that less time needs to be allocated to the physical development of the project, mistakes are easier to fix and less likely to occur, meaning a lower risk of deploying broken code out to the users and if something needed changing then it would be easy to substitute the code into a single place rather than have to deploy it out to every instance where it is used and produced.

This provided the application with a link to insert parameters to create requests on the client side, that we could store in the database tables.

I initially sketched out the basic layout of the program architecture and how the entities would overlap into different sections of the app. Information would be needed from the database to be displayed to the web views and the entities are how we transfer that data.

There was a section of code that is required to data map the information from the database into the calendar. The application worked perfectly with the code stepping through the entities individually, picking up the data we needed. The issue appeared when the users increased to more than ten because we did not account for scaling. The method that I implemented took far too long to return any data for the request. The limit for loading was set to 15-20 seconds, and the current search was taking well over two minutes. This was unacceptable and would prove useless in the long term as the company employs well over a thousand people, which would break the application. I resolved this loading issue by changing the method to step through a single entity, instead of jumping to different entities and wasting time.

I created a new entity called “Calendar Event” that was a collection of elements from all the other entities and only returned the specific information we required. This significantly reduced the loading time and retrieved the information in a much more effective way, that in turn was less taxing on the processing power. The entities carry the data through the application from the database to the web app and the web service.

Testing the Product

Once we pushed our work into the team repository the testers began to go through each page and see what elements could potentially fail and cause bugs. The first one that was raised was users were able to select a start date in the past and create a valid request. Also, in the same failure the tester spotted that the users start date could be after the end date. This was a simple fix of implementing some logic that limited the user to only select start dates past the current date, and if the end date is less than the start date it will throw an error.

The next problem was the calendar that we elected to use clashed with the CSS we used. The calendar primarily stores events as bars, that are then placed in the box that represents the date of the event. The issue appeared when multiple events were stored in a single day. Instead of appearing as a bar of ellipses and leaving the box the same size, the box stretched vertically to fit every single event inside it. The image of this occurring is below and we were unable to fix this issue within the time frame but that is in the section for future improvements.

One of the testers made himself a part of three teams to observe how the system would behave and he could indeed view other teams’ calendars. The issue was that because he was overlapping into other teams, the calendar duplicated events and showed them all on the same calendar. The system could not distinguish between the duplicates so it produced them all for every team. This is another issue that we are planning to fix for the future; the application is currently being used for a single team so this problem does not affect us at this time. Going forward when the application is released for the entire business, this will be one of the first issues that is addressed.

Another issue highlighted by the testers was the context menu didn’t direct you to the correct invite. When users right clicked on a specific request, it linked them to the pending request page or just the most recent invite. I found that the specific Id for the absence request wasn’t going to the right place. I was unable to get the Id to parse into the view and attach to the link when the specific event was clicked. I spent a lot of time on these issues but they were hindering the overall application completion time. I corrected some of the other issues in the context menu such as directing users straight to the create request page. Unfortunately, I couldn’t finish the specific event redirection so this task was also placed in the future fix section.

Conclusion

I completed all the major tasks I was set and the work was approved to be pushed up to the server. I gained a lot of experience with SQL, HTML, C# and the visual studio environment throughout the development of this application. If I had more time, I would have liked to have finished the work on my context menu as I feel it would have made the page more streamline and user friendly. I thoroughly enjoyed the independence I had with the project, even though we had some guidelines to stick to. My input and ideas were taken on board by the team and if there were ever instances where I had suggestions that were unable to be implemented; I was told why that particular idea was not feasible. These instances were primarily cosmetic and were unable to be implemented due to our tight time constraints. I learned a lot about the software development life cycle and how to be a more agile developer. My personal skills were developed and enhanced in line with business processes.
